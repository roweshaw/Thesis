# [Nitrogen and carbon/nitrogen dynamics in arbuscular mycorrhiza: the great unknown](https://dx.doi.org/10.1007/s00572-015-0627-6)

## [[A. Corrêa]]; [[C. Cruz]]; [[N. Ferrol]]

### 2015

## Abstract
Many studies have established that arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi transfer N to the host plant. However, the role and importance of arbuscular mycorrhiza (AM) in plant N nutrition is still uncertain, as are the C/N interactions within the symbiosis. Published reports provide differing, and often contradictory, results that are difficult to combine in a coherent framework. ==This review explores questions such as: What makes the difference between a positive and a negative effect of AM on plant N nutrition? Is the mycorrhizal N response (MNR) correlated to the mycorrhizal growth response (MGR), and how or under which conditions? Is the MNR effect on plant growth C mediated? Is plant C investment on fungal growth related to N needs or N benefit? How is the N for C trade between symbionts regulated? The patternless nature of current knowledge is made evident, and possible reasons for this are discussed==.

## Synopsis
Arbuscular mycorrhiza (AM) is a widespread symbiotic association formed between plants and fungi, and arguably one of the most important symbioses between living organisms.
AM effects on N uptake and plant growth have been observed to vary from negative to positive
In the absence of other stresses, positive MGR seems to always be a consequence of higher nutrient uptake by AM plants and relief of nutrient limitation to plant growth ([^Hamel_1991_a]; [^Ibijbijen_et+al_1996_a]</a>; [^Cruz_et+al_2004_a]; [^Jia_et+al_2004_a]</a>; [^Karagiannidis_et+al_2007_a]; Ruzicka et al 2011).
It has been previously hypothesized that negative effects on plant growth could occur as a consequence of fungal N retention ([^Smith_2011_b]), and recent studies have provided evidence supporting this hypothesis.
In a recent study, testing AM responses over a gradient of N availabilities, MNR, together with a synergy between N and Zn uptake, were found to explain MGR, and evidence was obtained that a fungal C drain was not associated with negative MGR (Corrêa et al 2014).
As discussed previously, several studies found no correlation between root colonization and mycorrhizal benefit, in terms of either MGR or MNR ([^Edathil_et+al_1996_a]; [^Graham_2000_a]; [^Ryan_2003_a]; [^Smith_et+al_2004_a]; van der Heijden et al 2006; [^Gao_et+al_2006_a]; [^Grace_et+al_2009_a]; [^Busby_et+al_2011_a]; [^Aleklett_2012_a]; Büscher et al 2012; [^Sikes_et+al_2012_a]; [^Fellbaum_et+al_2014_a]).
As observed for P ([^Treseder_2004_a]</a>), increased N supply often reduces the percentage of mycorrhizal root colonization ([^Jensen_1980_a]; [^Chambers_et+al_1980_a]; [^Hays_et+al_1982_a]; [^Hepper_1983_a]; [^Johnson_1984_a]; [^Cuenca_1994_a]; [^Bressan_2001_a]; Miller et al 2002; Jackson et al 2002; Johnson et al 2003; [^Treseder_2004_a]</a>; [^Jia_et+al_2004_a]</a>; [^Blanke_et+al_2005_a]</a>; Becerra et al 2007; [^Hodge_2010_a]; [^Liu_et+al_2012_a]; [^Nouri_et+al_2014_a]; Corrêa et al 2014), and [^Tu_et+al_2006_a]), using 13CO2, observed decreased belowground C allocation in AM plants with increased N fertilization again suggesting changed C allocation towards AM fungi with the N supply.
Studies have revealed correlations between root colonization and plant tissue N concentrations that were positive ([^Tu_et+al_2006_a]), negative (Miller et al 2002; [^Blanke_et+al_2005_a]</a>), or neutral ([^Ibijbijen_et+al_1996_a]</a>).
If so, analyzing them together is not likely to lead to a common, consistent conclusion

## Findings
- In a study of C allocation into ERM in mixed flax and sorghum cultures using stable isotope compositions, Walder et al (2013) found that flax allocated much less carbon to the AM fungus but gained up to 94 % of the N and P that it provided

##  Confirmation of earlier findings
- Differences in N uptake between AM and NM plants may be directly responsible for negative MGR. It has been previously hypothesized that negative effects on plant growth could occur as a consequence of fungal N retention ([^Smith_2011_b]), and ==recent studies have provided evidence supporting this hypothesis==

## Contributions
- Currently available data do not allow construction of a coherent common framework for, or even provide consistent answers to, the main questions concerning N and C/N dynamics in AM. This disarray may derive from a number of factors. One possible cause of the disparate observations is that the reported experiments studied a wide variety of different plant and fungal species, and even plant genera (Table 1). A metaanalysis revealed the host plant functional group to be one of the factors that best explained variations in MGR, together with N fertilization ([^Hoeksema_et+al_2010_a]). This has been repeatedly pointed out as one of the problems in mycorrhizal research, but it of course reflects the fact that AM fungi establish symbioses in a variety of habitats with a wide variety of plant species with various life strategies and resource needs. It is possible that AM interactions between different plant and fungal partners follow different basic premises, and so establish different dynamics. If so, analyzing them together is not likely to lead to a common, consistent conclusion.

## Future work
- Further studies are needed using other experimental systems, with other AMF and, namely, with plants presenting more positive responses to AM than rice.
- Further research is necessary to determine the underlying reasons for this.
